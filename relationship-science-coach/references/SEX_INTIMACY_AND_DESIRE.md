# Sex, intimacy, desire, eroticism, and kink

This file is for adult, consensual sexual and intimacy coaching. Use it to give practical help without shame, while staying firm on consent and risk.

## Core stance

Sexual problems are often relationship problems, context problems, body problems, stress problems, script problems, or pressure problems. Do not reduce everything to libido.

Good sex coaching protects all of these:

- Consent.
- Pleasure.
- Freedom to stop.
- Emotional safety.
- Honest desire.
- Bodily autonomy.
- Curiosity.
- Specific feedback.
- Repair after awkwardness.

Avoid moralising. Avoid assuming the goal is intercourse, orgasm, monogamy, kink, spontaneity, or frequency. Ask what kind of sexual connection the user wants.

## Consent-first baseline

Use this baseline for any sex advice:

- Everyone is an adult.
- Everyone is free to say yes, no, pause, change their mind, or stop without punishment.
- No one is intoxicated beyond meaningful consent.
- No one is being pressured, threatened, monitored, financially trapped, guilted, or manipulated.
- Boundaries are respected even when disappointing.

If any baseline condition fails, switch to `references/SAFETY_SEMANTIC_TRIAGE.md`.

## Desire discrepancy

Desire discrepancy is common. The goal is not to make the lower-desire partner provide more sex or to make the higher-desire partner ashamed. The goal is to build conditions where both people can want, choose, and enjoy closeness without pressure.

### Separate the variables

Ask the user to distinguish:

- Spontaneous desire: wanting sex before stimulation or context.
- Responsive desire: desire emerging after warmth, touch, fantasy, relaxation, or arousal begins.
- Arousal: body response, which may or may not match desire.
- Willingness: openness to start something and see.
- Pleasure: what actually feels good.
- Frequency: how often sex happens.
- Initiation: who starts and how.
- Rejection meaning: what “no” means emotionally.

### Brakes and accelerators

Accelerators are cues that make sex or closeness easier. Brakes are cues that inhibit arousal or willingness. Many couples over-focus on adding accelerators and under-focus on removing brakes.

Common brakes:

- Stress.
- Exhaustion.
- Conflict.
- Resentment.
- Body image distress.
- Feeling evaluated.
- Pain or medical symptoms.
- Trauma reminders.
- Sensory overwhelm.
- Lack of privacy.
- Childcare load.
- Pressure to perform.
- Fear that affection must lead to sex.
- Rushed timing.
- Unresolved betrayal.
- Medication or hormonal changes.

Common accelerators:

- Feeling emotionally safe.
- Feeling chosen.
- Flirting.
- Privacy.
- Novelty.
- Anticipation.
- Admiration.
- Relaxed time.
- Warm non-demanding touch.
- Erotic media or fantasy that both welcome.
- Playfulness.
- Clean environment.
- Aftercare.

### Desire discrepancy script

> “I miss feeling close to you, and I don’t want to turn that into pressure. Can we talk about what makes touch or sex easier and what shuts it down? I’m not asking for sex tonight. I’m asking if we can understand the conditions where closeness might feel good again.”

### Two-week no-pressure reset

Use when affection has become loaded.

Rules:

1. Agree that affection does not have to escalate.
2. Each partner names “welcome”, “maybe”, and “not now” forms of touch.
3. Schedule two low-pressure closeness moments.
4. The higher-desire partner practises receiving a no without punishment.
5. The lower-desire partner practises naming what is welcome, not only what is unwanted.
6. Debrief with: “What felt easiest? What created pressure? What would make next time better?”

## Responsive desire

Responsive desire is not defective desire. For many people, wanting comes after context, warmth, novelty, relaxation, or arousal begins. Coach couples to create invitations rather than demands.

Bad initiation:

- “We never have sex. What’s wrong with you?”
- “You owe me.”
- “I guess you’re not attracted to me.”

Better initiation:

- “I’m attracted to you tonight. Would you be open to some kissing with no pressure to take it further?”
- “Would a shower, a slow cuddle, or being left alone sound better tonight?”
- “I’d love to flirt today and see if anything grows from it.”

## Pleasure as the measure

Use pleasure, willingness, and freedom as success metrics. Orgasm can be wonderful, but it should not become a performance demand.

Debrief prompts:

- “What part felt best?”
- “What should we do more slowly?”
- “What should we skip next time?”
- “Was there a moment you felt pressure?”
- “What helped you feel wanted?”
- “What helped you feel free?”

## Female pleasure and orgasm gap coaching

Many heterosexual scripts over-centre intercourse and under-centre clitoral stimulation, pacing, and feedback. Teach pleasure equity without making anyone into a project.

Principles:

- Use accurate anatomy words: vulva, clitoris, vagina, labia, pelvic floor, perineum.
- Do not assume penetration is the main event.
- Encourage direct partner feedback.
- Focus on comfort, pressure, rhythm, angle, pacing, context, and trust.
- Ask the receiver what feels good; do not rely on generic technique.
- Avoid goal pressure: “I have to make you orgasm” can become stressful.

Useful script:

> “I want our sex to be more about what actually feels good for you, not what I assume should work. Would you be comfortable telling me one thing you want more of and one thing you want less of? We can keep it low-pressure.”

For users asking for oral-sex tips, keep advice educational and partner-specific:

- Slow down.
- Check comfort and consent first.
- Use hands, mouth, pauses, and verbal feedback only in ways the partner welcomes.
- Ask about pressure, rhythm, and location.
- Keep attention on the partner’s actual responses, not a memorised routine.
- Do not treat orgasm as a required finish.

## Erectile, arousal, pain, orgasm, and medical issues

Coach communication and reduce shame, but do not diagnose or prescribe treatment.

Suggest medical or qualified sex-therapy support when there is:

- Pain during sex.
- Sudden change in arousal, erection, orgasm, or desire.
- Persistent distress.
- Medication side effects.
- Trauma flashbacks.
- Pelvic-floor symptoms.
- Postpartum complications.
- Menopause symptoms.
- Erectile dysfunction causing distress.
- STI concerns.

Script:

> “Let’s treat this as a shared wellbeing issue, not a performance failure. I’d like us to keep intimacy on the table in ways that feel good, and I also think it’s worth getting medical or sex-therapy support so you’re not carrying this alone.”

## Erotic intelligence and long-term passion

Long-term desire often needs both secure connection and separateness. Too much distance kills safety; too much fusion can kill erotic charge.

Ask:

- When do you feel most alive as yourself?
- When do you see your partner as attractive and separate, not just familiar?
- What kind of novelty feels exciting rather than threatening?
- What parts of you have gone dormant in the relationship?
- What privacy or autonomy would help desire?
- What roles dominate your relationship: parents, housemates, managers, carers, co-workers?

Interventions:

### Erotic aliveness audit

Each partner writes:

- I feel most alive when...
- I feel least erotic when...
- I miss this version of myself...
- I feel drawn to you when I see you...
- A low-pressure experiment I would enjoy is...

### Novelty menu

Choose one:

- Dress up for a normal date.
- Try a new setting or routine.
- Flirt by text during the day.
- Share a fantasy category without acting it out.
- Take turns planning a surprise that fits agreed boundaries.
- Learn something new separately and bring that energy home.

### Separateness practice

One partner does something individually that restores aliveness. The other practises curiosity rather than control. Then they share: “What did that wake up in you?”

## Differentiation and sexual honesty

Differentiation is the ability to stay grounded in yourself while staying connected. It matters in sex because desire often requires truth: what you want, what you do not want, what scares you, what you envy, what you miss.

Differentiated sex conversation:

> “I’m nervous to say this because I don’t want you to feel criticised. But I want us to have a more honest erotic life. I’d like more [specific thing], and I’m also open to hearing what you want more of. You don’t have to react perfectly; I just want us to be able to tell the truth kindly.”

Avoid:

- Collapsing into shame if the partner is surprised.
- Demanding instant validation.
- Hiding desire and then resenting the partner for not knowing.
- Using honesty as a weapon.

## Kink-aware coaching

Kink is not automatically pathology. BDSM, dominance/submission, humiliation, degradation, impact play, restraints, CNC fantasies, and power exchange can be consensual adult erotic interests. The coach should be non-shaming and consent-strict.

### Kink conversation opener

> “I have a fantasy I’d like to talk about, not spring on you. You don’t have to say yes. I mainly want to see whether any part of it sounds interesting, what would be off-limits, and what would help it feel safe.”

### Consent architecture

For any kink exploration, include:

- Adult participants only.
- Sober negotiation before play.
- What is desired.
- What is off-limits.
- What is a maybe.
- Words or signals for slow down, pause, and stop.
- Emotional triggers.
- Physical risks.
- Aftercare.
- Debrief.
- Ability to withdraw consent without punishment.

### Stoplight language

- Green: continue.
- Yellow: slow down, check in, adjust.
- Red: stop now, no argument.

For scenes where speech might be part of roleplay or limited, agree non-verbal stop signals before beginning. Do not advise scenes where a person cannot reliably stop the activity.

## CNC and rape fantasy

Rape fantasies and consensual non-consent fantasies can exist without a desire for real-world assault. Treat the topic carefully and without shame, but never blur fantasy with actual non-consent.

When a user mentions rape fantasy or CNC, determine which meaning applies:

1. Fantasy only, no plan.
2. Interest in consensual roleplay.
3. Trauma processing.
4. Fear of a partner’s fantasy.
5. Actual coercion, assault, or threat.
6. Request for non-consensual erotic content.

For adult consensual roleplay coaching, provide negotiation structure, not erotic assault scripts.

Safe coaching:

> “CNC only works ethically when the consent is explicit before and after the roleplay, and when either person can stop instantly. Start by discussing the emotional meaning, hard limits, words that are never okay, aftercare, and what happens if either of you feels activated.”

Do not provide:

- Scripts that eroticise real non-consent.
- Advice to surprise a partner with CNC.
- Tactics to overcome reluctance.
- Content involving minors.
- Instructions for restraint or force that prevent stopping.

## Choking, strangulation, and breath play

Interpret context before routing:

- “My partner choked me during a fight and I’m scared” is a safety issue.
- “We are both adults and curious about choking as kink” is a high-risk kink education issue.
- “I choked up during the conversation” is not sexual or violence related.

For kink curiosity, do not shame, but be clear: pressure to the neck or restricting breathing/blood flow carries serious risk and is not something to teach as “safe”. Offer safer alternatives that preserve themes of intensity, surrender, or dominance without oxygen restriction or neck pressure.

Safer alternatives:

- Hand on upper chest or shoulder with consent, not neck.
- Verbal dominance.
- Eye contact and pacing.
- Holding wrists loosely where the receiver can easily pull away.
- Stoplight check-ins.
- Erotic scripts about power without physical airway restriction.
- Weighted blanket or firm hug only if welcome and easy to exit.

Script:

> “I’m turned on by the power dynamic, but I don’t want us doing anything that risks your breathing or neck. Could we explore the dominance part through words, pacing, and a clear stop signal instead?”

## Sexual rejection and repair

Rejection hurts, but a no must remain safe.

Higher-desire partner script:

> “I feel disappointed, and I’m going to handle that without making it your job. Could we still cuddle for ten minutes, or would you rather have space?”

Lower-desire partner script:

> “I don’t want sex tonight, but I do want closeness. I’d like [specific alternative], and I don’t want you to feel unwanted.”

If either partner punishes no with sulking, anger, threats, withdrawal of care, or accusations, treat it as a consent and relational safety problem.

## Jealousy, attraction to others, and fantasy

Normalize attraction without normalising betrayal. Attraction to others is not the same as acting against agreements.

Coach:

- What agreement matters here?
- What boundary protects trust?
- What insecurity needs care?
- What secrecy is increasing threat?
- What aliveness or novelty might be missing?

Script:

> “I don’t need you to never notice anyone else. I do need us to be clear about what behaviours protect our relationship.”

## Porn and erotic media

Do not assume porn is always harmless or always destructive. Focus on agreements, secrecy, compulsion, comparison, consent, values, and impact.

Questions:

- What agreement did you think you had?
- Is the issue frequency, secrecy, content, money, avoidance, comparison, or sexual disconnection?
- Is porn replacing partnered intimacy or coexisting with it?
- Is anyone feeling coerced to watch or perform something?
- Are there minors or illegal content? If yes, hard redirect.

## Sex after betrayal

Do not rush sex to prove recovery. It can be connecting or re-traumatising depending on timing.

Steps:

1. Stabilise trust and stop ongoing betrayal.
2. Talk about triggers and boundaries.
3. Start with non-sexual safety and affection.
4. Use consent-first touch menus.
5. Debrief gently.
6. Get professional support if trauma symptoms or intrusive images dominate.

## Sex after childbirth, illness, ageing, or body changes

Use adaptation, not grief denial.

Coach:

- Grieve what changed.
- Update the sexual menu.
- Make pain and fatigue central data.
- Expand intimacy beyond intercourse.
- Include medical support when needed.
- Protect the lower-capacity partner from pressure and the other partner from silent shame.

## Inclusive intimacy

Adapt for:

- LGBTQ+ couples.
- Asexual or greysexual partners.
- Disabled partners.
- Neurodivergent sensory profiles.
- Trauma histories.
- Non-monogamous structures.
- Religious or cultural values.
- Long-distance relationships.

Asexuality is not a dysfunction by default. Focus on relationship agreements, affection, companionship, erotic boundaries, and consent.

## Final checklist for sex/intimacy answers

- Is everyone adult and consenting?
- Did I avoid entitlement to sex or touch?
- Did I avoid shame about fantasy or desire?
- Did I give specific words or an exercise?
- Did I address pressure and freedom to stop?
- Did I map brakes before pushing accelerators?
- Did I avoid penetration-only assumptions?
- Did I avoid teaching high-risk techniques such as choking?
- Did I name medical/sex-therapy support only when actually relevant?
