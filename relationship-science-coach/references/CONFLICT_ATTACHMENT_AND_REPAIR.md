# Conflict, attachment, repair, and trust

## Core frame

Conflict is rarely just the topic. It is usually a topic plus a pattern plus a nervous-system state plus a meaning. The coach should intervene at the lowest layer that is currently blocking progress.

Layers:

1. **Safety:** Is anyone afraid, coerced, monitored, threatened, or harmed?
2. **Physiology:** Is either person flooded, shut down, intoxicated, or unable to listen?
3. **Pattern:** Is there criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling, pursue-withdraw, blame, or escalation?
4. **Attachment meaning:** Does the fight mean “I don’t matter”, “I’m trapped”, “I’ll be abandoned”, “I’m a failure”, “I can never get it right”, or “I’m alone”?
5. **Solvable problem:** What practical agreement is needed?

Do not try to solve layer 5 when layers 1 to 4 are active.

## Gottman conflict tools

### Gentle or soft start-up

Use when the user wants to raise a concern and there are no safety red flags.

Template:

> “When [specific event] happened, I felt [emotion] because [meaning or need]. I’d like [positive need]. Could we [small specific request]?”

Examples:

- “When we left the dishes until morning three nights this week, I felt overwhelmed. I need the kitchen to feel manageable before bed. Could we do a ten-minute reset together after dinner?”
- “When you looked at your phone while I was telling you about work, I felt unimportant. I’d love ten minutes of full attention after dinner. Could we put phones away for that?”

Bad version:

- “You never help.”
- “You don’t care about me.”
- “I guess I’m the only adult here.”

### Four Horsemen antidotes

Criticism attacks character. Antidote: specific complaint plus positive need.

Contempt communicates disgust or superiority. Antidote: appreciation, respect, and behaviour-specific complaint. If contempt is frequent, work on resentment inventory and fondness before problem-solving.

Defensiveness refuses responsibility. Antidote: own one small part, even if the whole accusation is unfair.

Stonewalling shuts down. Antidote: time-bound break and reliable return.

Coach output should include one replacement sentence.

### Repair attempts

Repair is any move that interrupts escalation and reorients toward connection. Good repair is brief, specific, and not self-protective.

Repair phrases:

- “I’m getting defensive. Let me try again.”
- “That came out harshly. What I meant was...”
- “I care more about us than winning this point.”
- “I need a break so I don’t say something cruel. I’ll come back at 8:30.”
- “Can I summarise what I heard before I respond?”

Teach users to accept repairs, not just make them. A repair attempt can fail if it is sarcastic, vague, controlling, or followed by more blame.

### Flooding and time-outs

Flooding is a body state where listening and empathy are impaired. When flooded, more words often make things worse.

Time-out plan:

1. Name it without blame: “I’m flooded and I’m not going to be useful if we continue.”
2. Set return time: “I’ll come back in 30 minutes.”
3. Do calming actions: walk, breathe, shower, stretch, listen to music, write feelings without sending.
4. Avoid escalation behaviours: alcohol, substances, rage texting, rehearsing counterattacks, doom-scrolling, calling allies to build a case.
5. Return: “I’m back. The part I understand is... The part I need you to understand is...”

If one partner uses breaks to avoid every conversation, add a reliability rule: break length and return time are mandatory.

## EFT and attachment-cycle tools

### Cycle map

Use when the couple repeats a pattern where one person pursues and one withdraws, or both escalate.

Map:

- Trigger: what happened.
- Partner A’s protective move: criticise, text repeatedly, demand, shut down, fix, defend, withdraw.
- Partner B’s protective move.
- Meaning each person makes.
- Primary emotion underneath.
- Need underneath.
- New move.

Example:

- Trigger: Partner comes home late without texting.
- A’s move: interrogates and criticises.
- B’s move: withdraws and says “nothing I do is enough”.
- A’s meaning: “I don’t matter.”
- B’s meaning: “I’m failing and will be attacked.”
- A’s primary emotion: fear/loneliness.
- B’s primary emotion: shame/overwhelm.
- New move: A asks for reassurance and text agreement; B acknowledges impact and commits to text if late.

### Vulnerable reframe

Secondary move: “You’re selfish.”

Vulnerable reframe: “When I didn’t hear from you, I felt unimportant and scared. I need to know I matter even when plans change.”

Secondary move: “Stop smothering me.”

Vulnerable reframe: “When the conversation gets intense, I feel trapped and afraid I’ll disappoint you. I need a slower pace and a reliable way to return.”

### Accessibility, responsiveness, engagement

Use these as plain questions:

- Accessibility: “Can I reach you emotionally when I need you?”
- Responsiveness: “Do you show that my feelings matter?”
- Engagement: “Are you present and involved with me?”

A bonding conversation should make one of these stronger.

## Attachment coaching without labels as weapons

Do not say “Your partner is avoidant, so...” unless the user supplied that frame and it is useful. Prefer: “The behaviour looks like distancing under stress.”

### Anxious activation

Common moves:

- Repeated texting.
- Reassurance seeking.
- Testing.
- Jealous interrogation.
- Catastrophic interpretations.
- Protest anger.

Coaching:

- Name the need clearly.
- Reduce protest behaviour.
- Self-soothe before asking.
- Ask one concrete request.
- Create a response-time agreement.

Script:

> “I’m feeling activated and I know repeated texts push you away. I’m going to take 20 minutes to settle. What I need is a clear time we’ll reconnect tonight.”

### Avoidant distancing

Common moves:

- Shutdown.
- Minimising.
- Delaying indefinitely.
- Intellectualising.
- “I don’t know” as escape.
- Working, gaming, scrolling, or disappearing.

Coaching:

- Name overwhelm without contempt.
- Offer a return time.
- Give one sentence of reassurance before taking space.
- Practise staying present in small doses.

Script:

> “I’m overwhelmed, not uncaring. I need 30 minutes, and I will come back. The one thing I can say now is that this matters to me.”

### Fearful/disorganised patterns

Common moves:

- Wanting closeness and fearing it.
- Escalating then disappearing.
- Suspicion plus longing.
- Trauma-linked reactions.

Coaching:

- Slow everything down.
- Make requests small.
- Use safety and predictability.
- Encourage trauma-informed professional support if reactions are intense or disabling.

## Connection before analysis

Use when talking has become the fight.

Plan:

1. Announce a no-analysis reset: “For the next 20 minutes we’re not solving us. We’re just being kind.”
2. Choose one low-demand connection: walk, tea, shared task, music, sitting together, appreciation.
3. Each person names one appreciated behaviour from the last week.
4. Stop. Do not sneak in the complaint.
5. Later, discuss one issue for ten minutes using listener summaries.

This is not avoidance. It is rebuilding enough safety for conversation to work.

## Trust repair

Trust repair is not reassurance alone. It needs accountability, impact, observable behaviour, and time.

### Accountable apology

Structure:

1. Behaviour: “I did X.”
2. Impact: “I can see it affected you by Y.”
3. Responsibility: “That is on me.”
4. Context without excuse: “I was stressed, but that does not make it okay.”
5. Change: “I will do Z.”
6. Choice: “You do not have to reassure me right now.”

Example:

> “I lied about texting my ex. That broke the agreement we made, and I understand why you feel unsafe with me. I was avoiding conflict, but that is not an excuse. I’m going to be honest even when it creates discomfort, and I’m willing to answer questions tonight for 30 minutes without defending.”

### Transparency without surveillance

Transparency can repair trust; surveillance can create coercion or obsession. Coach agreements that are time-limited, specific, mutually understood, and reviewable.

Better:

- “For the next month, I’ll proactively tell you if I have contact with X.”
- “We’ll review boundaries every Sunday.”
- “I’ll answer reasonable questions without trickle-truth.”

Riskier:

- Permanent phone tracking.
- Demands for all passwords.
- Secret monitoring.
- Testing or traps.

### Affair or betrayal recovery

Do not rush forgiveness. Phases:

1. Stabilise and stop ongoing betrayal.
2. Full accountability and factual clarity appropriate to the couple.
3. Impact listening.
4. Boundary agreements.
5. Meaning-making and prevention.
6. Reconnection and, if desired, erotic rebuilding.

Refer out when there is trauma, coercion, repeated betrayal, severe dysregulation, legal issues, or safety concerns.

## Perpetual problems and compromise

Some problems are not solved because they are tied to identity, values, family culture, temperament, neurodivergence, money history, religion, sex, parenting, independence, or security.

Coach the dream, not the surface position.

Interview prompts:

- “What does your position protect?”
- “What fear comes up if you give ground?”
- “Where did you learn this mattered?”
- “What would respecting your dream look like, even if your partner does not fully agree?”
- “What part is flexible?”
- “What part feels core?”

Compromise circles:

- Inner circle: core needs and values.
- Outer circle: preferences and negotiables.
- Overlap: experiment for two weeks.

## Neurodivergence-aware adaptations

Do not assume eye contact, rapid emotional labelling, spontaneous verbal processing, or unstructured conversation works for everyone.

Adaptations:

- Written prompts before discussion.
- Timers.
- One topic only.
- Sensory-friendly environment.
- Clear turn-taking.
- Concrete requests.
- Repair after shutdown without shaming shutdown.
- Lower ambiguity in flirting and intimacy initiation.

## Non-monogamy-aware adaptations

Do not assume monogamy. Ask what agreement structure the relationship has: monogamous, open, polyamorous, swinging, relationship anarchy, undecided, or repairing broken agreements.

Coaching principles:

- Consent and explicit agreements matter more than structure.
- Jealousy is information, not a command.
- Do not frame a consensual non-monogamous structure as automatically unhealthy.
- Broken agreements are betrayals regardless of structure.
- Use calendars, safer-sex agreements, disclosure expectations, and metamour boundaries where relevant.

## Household and cohabitation conflict

Common issues: chores, money, tidiness, sleep, in-laws, parenting, mental load, screens, work stress.

Useful rule: make the invisible visible.

Exercise:

1. List tasks.
2. Include planning, noticing, emotional labour, and follow-through.
3. Mark current owner.
4. Mark desired owner.
5. Agree minimum standard.
6. Review in one week.

Do not use “helping” language when both partners own the household.

## Final answer checklist for this domain

- Did I make the issue behavioural and specific?
- Did I avoid diagnosing the absent partner?
- Did I identify physiology/cycle before content?
- Did I give a script or exercise?
- Did I include timing and repair?
- Did I avoid “just communicate”?
- Did I name when not to attempt the conversation?
